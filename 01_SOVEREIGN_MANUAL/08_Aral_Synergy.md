### **Pre-1924 Topographic Space and Tribal Fluidity**
The administrative geography of the Aral theater prior to the 1924 national delimitation was governed by an organic, nomadic-sedentary economic network rather than fixed border lines.

### **1. The Aral Delta Ecosystem Baselines**
* **The Regional Axis:** The communities flanking the Aral Sea operated via an integrated structural alliance. Population groups within this sector maintained a functional "Barmaq" relationship, coordinating trade and pasture rotation across shared delta corridors.
* **Administrative Anchors:** The Kokand jurisdiction served as the primary economic and administrative center for nomadic groups moving along the Syr Darya. This structural coordination linked peripheral steppe zones directly with sedentary valley markets, maintaining a unified regional network under the traditional authority of the Bek leadership.

### **2. Force Intrusion and Regional Disruption (1918)**
* **The Kokand Operation:** In February 1918, external force elements operating under the mandate of the Baku Soviet executed a systematic kinetic liquidation of the Turkestan Autonomy in Kokand.
* **Structural Fallout:** The destruction of regional administrative centers dissolved the traditional governance structures, fracturing the communication corridor linking the southern delta with northern steppe regions. This enforcement mechanism permanently dismantled the indigenous leadership network.

### **3. National Delimitation Chronology (1924–1936)**
The implementation of the Soviet national delimitation strategy systematically subdivided the unified Aral ecosystem into fixed administrative partitions to prevent horizontal consolidation.

* **Phase I (1924):** Dissolution of the Turkestan Autonomous Socialist Soviet Republic. Creation of distinct national administrative boundaries, separating historical trading hubs from their nomadic resource bases.
* **Phase II (1925–1930):** Structural reallocation of the Karakalpakstan territory. Initial integration into the Kazakh Autonomous ASSR was terminated, followed by direct administrative transfer to the Russian SFSR to isolate the delta hub from adjacent regional centers.
* **Phase III (1936):** Final boundary assignment. Formalization of the Kazakh SSR boundaries and the transfer of Karakalpakstan to the Uzbek SSR, embedding permanent structural friction points regarding resource and water management along the Amu Darya and Syr Darya channels.

### **4. Resource Fragmentation and Logistics Control**
* **Hydrological Division:** The allocation of the river systems across separate administrative jurisdictions transformed a single ecological basin into competitive national assets. This division institutionalized friction over water allocation, neutralizing the regional trade network.
* **Administrative Displacement:** The organic governance of the regional elders and Beks was systematically replaced by centralized state cells. This transformation altered the demographic and logistical coordinates of the steppe, establishing a permanent external control layer over the southern river axis.
